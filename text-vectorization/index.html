Text vectorization

How text becomes numbers: classic representations from count-based bags to learned embeddings.

Text vectorization means turning text into numeric vectors so models can process it. The methods below differ in how they count, weight, or learn those numbers—and in whether they capture word order, rarity, or meaning.

Bag of Words TF‑IDF Word2Vec GloVe FastText

Bag of Words (BoW)

Idea: Count how many times each word from a shared vocabulary appears in a document. Order is ignored.

Corpus:
D1 - "I love NLP"
D2 - "I love AI"

Vocabulary: [I, love, NLP, AI]

Vectors:
D1 → [1, 1, 1, 0]
D2 → [1, 1, 0, 1]

Advantages

  • Simple and easy to implement
  • Fine for baseline classification and small data

Disadvantages

  • Ignores word order (e.g. “love I NLP” can match “I love NLP”)
  • No real notion of context or meaning
  • Sparse, high-dimensional vectors (many zeros)

When it shines

  • Basic text classification (e.g. spam vs not spam)
  • Small, controlled vocabularies

TF‑IDF (term frequency – inverse document frequency)

Idea: Scale raw counts: frequent words in one document (TF) matter, but words that appear in every document get down‑weighted (IDF).

  • TF — how often a term appears in a document (normalized in practice).
  • IDF — rare across the corpus → higher weight; very common (e.g. “the”, “is”) → lower.

Intuition

  • “food” in every document → not very discriminating (low final weight if truly ubiquitous).
  • “good” / “bad” in reviews → can carry more weight when contrastive.
  • Discriminative, somewhat rare terms often rank higher than stopwords.

Not all words are equally important. TF‑IDF downplays globally common terms and upweights terms that are more specific to a document or a small set of documents.

Pros

  • Better than raw BoW for search, ranking, and “what is this document about”
  • Reduces noise from function words (when they appear in many documents)

Cons

  • Still no true semantic similarity between different words (synonyms are separate dimensions)
  • Remains relatively sparse; no deep context

When it shines

  • Search, keywording, and classical IR baselines
  • Document similarity and feature engineering for tabular + text pipelines

Word2Vec

Instead of hand counts only, learn dense vectors from data: similar usage patterns in context → similar vectors in space (cosine distance often used).

king  → [0.25, -0.10, ...]
queen → [0.27, -0.12, ...]

Nearby vectors often reflect related meanings or syntactic relations (famous “vector arithmetic” demos).

Training approaches

  • CBOW — predict a word from its context window
  • Skip-gram — predict context from the center word

Illustrative co-occurrence (conceptual)

  • “love” may appear near “food”, “hotel” in a sentiment corpus
  • “good” and “bad” can both appear near “food” with different class labels in supervised tasks
love → [0.2, 0.8]
food → [0.3, 0.7]
good → [0.9, 0.1]
bad  → [0.85, 0.15]

Pros

  • Dense vectors (lower dimension than huge sparse BoW, in practice)
  • Captures analogies and similarity better than count-only methods

Cons

  • One vector per word type — polysemy (“bank” of river vs money) is a single point
  • Out-of-vocabulary words need a separate strategy (subword models, later)

GloVe (Global Vectors)

Builds on global word co-occurrence statistics across the whole corpus (not only local window sampling). Combines the strengths of count-based and prediction-based ideas in one framework.

  • “food” might co-occur with: love, hotel, good, bad, … in global counts
  • “good” might cluster with certain sentiment contexts; “bad” with others

Uses global matrix information, not only sliding-window SGD in isolation.

Pros

  • Strong, stable pretrained word vectors for many languages and domains
  • Often a solid default in classical NLP before contextual transformers

Cons

  • Still a single vector per word; sense disambiguation is limited
  • New/rare words need OOV handling (as with vanilla Word2Vec)

When it shines

  • Pretrained embeddings in pipelines, feature layers, and legacy systems
  • When you want interpretable, fixed-size word features without training from scratch

FastText

Represents words (and in some settings, whole text) as sums of subword n-gram vectors. Rare words, typos, and morphological variants can still get reasonable vectors.

hotel → ho, hot, ote, tel, hotel, …
food  → fo, foo, ood, food, …

Pros

  • Better coverage for unseen or rare word forms (via subwords)
  • Multilingual and noisy text can benefit; morphologically rich languages too

Cons

  • Somewhat more engineering and memory than a pure word-level model
  • Not sentence-level context like a Transformer—still (mostly) type-level in spirit

When it shines

  • Noisy user text, short snippets, and multilingual corpora
  • When you need OOV behavior without a huge lexicon
